The Asian Dollarbird, scientifically known as Eurystomus orientalis, is a bird species native to parts of Asia and Australasia. These birds are primarily found in regions such as India, Southeast Asia, Australia, and the Pacific Islands. Asian Dollarbirds inhabit various habitats, including woodlands, forests, savannas, and urban areas, where they forage for insects, particularly flying insects such as butterflies and dragonflies. They are known for their distinctive appearance, characterized by their striking blue-green plumage, black facial mask, and broad, flattened bills.

1. Plumage: The Asian Dollarbird displays striking blue-green plumage on its upperparts, complemented by paler underparts and a black facial mask extending from its bill to its eyes.
2. Habitat: These birds are native to parts of Asia and Australasia, where they primarily inhabit a variety of habitats, including woodlands, forests, savannas, and urban areas.
3. Foraging Behavior: Asian Dollarbirds are insectivorous birds, feeding primarily on flying insects such as butterflies, moths, dragonflies, and beetles, which they catch in mid-air using their broad, flattened bills.
4. Migration: They are migratory birds, undertaking long-distance migrations between their breeding grounds in temperate regions and their wintering grounds in tropical regions.
5. Vocalizations: Asian Dollarbirds produce a series of loud, melodious calls and whistles, which they use for communication within their social groups and to establish territories during the breeding season.
