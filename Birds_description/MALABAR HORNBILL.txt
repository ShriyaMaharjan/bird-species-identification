The Malabar Pied Hornbill (Anthracoceros coronatus) is a bird species native to the Western Ghats and associated hills of southwestern India. Originating from this region, the Malabar Pied Hornbill inhabits evergreen forests and wooded areas, displaying a preference for hilly and mountainous landscapes. Recognized for its distinctive appearance and behaviors, this hornbill species contributes to the rich avian diversity of the Western Ghats.

1. The Malabar Pied Hornbill exhibits a striking black and white plumage, with a black head, neck, and wings contrasting sharply with a white belly and tail. This bold coloration contributes to its unique and visually appealing appearance.

2. With a sizeable, downward-curved bill featuring a casque on top, the Malabar Pied Hornbill possesses a distinctive facial structure. The casque is more prominent in males and plays a role in visual displays and communication within the species.

3. These hornbills are arboreal and primarily frugivorous, with a diet consisting of various fruits, figs, and berries. Their foraging behavior involves searching for food in the canopy of trees, contributing to seed dispersal in the forest ecosystem.

4. Malabar Pied Hornbills are known for their loud, cackling calls and distinctive vocalizations that resonate through the forest. These calls are utilized for communication within the species, particularly during courtship displays and to establish territory.

5. Breeding pairs of Malabar Pied Hornbills engage in cavity-nesting behavior, selecting tree hollows for nesting sites. The female seals herself inside the chosen cavity during the nesting period, leaving only a narrow slit through which the male can pass food, highlighting the unique reproductive strategy of this hornbill species.
