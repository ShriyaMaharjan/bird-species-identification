The Black-capped Chickadee (Poecile atricapillus) is a small songbird native to North America, belonging to the family Paridae. Its range spans across Canada, parts of Alaska, and down into the northern United States, including regions such as the Pacific Northwest, the Great Lakes, and the Northeast. These birds inhabit various forested areas, including deciduous and mixed woodlands, as well as urban and suburban environments. They are well-known for their distinctive black cap and bib, white cheeks, and grayish-white underparts.

1. Size: Black-capped Chickadees are relatively small birds, measuring around 4.7 to 5.9 inches (12 to 15 centimeters) in length.
2. Plumage: They have a striking appearance, with a black cap and bib that contrast sharply against their white cheeks and grayish-white underparts.
3. Vocalizations: One of their most recognizable features is their distinct call, often described as "chick-a-dee-dee-dee," from which they derive their name.
4. Behavior: These birds are known for their curious and bold behavior, often approaching bird feeders and human observers with confidence.
5. Range: Black-capped Chickadees have a wide distribution across North America, found in various habitats from forests to parks and suburban gardens.
